A high-definition CCTV camera installed deep in the Linux Kernel. Uses eBPF technology to run without modifying the kernel.

Scenario 1: Linux Host Defense & Auto-Remediation

Background & Challenge

Linux is the bedrock of cloud infrastructure. Its security goals are Confidentiality (no leaks) and Integrity (no tampering). Without protection, hackers use Rootkits (kernel backdoors) to hide, even deleting logs to erase tracks, leaving enterprises unaware of data theft for months.

MTTD (Mean Time To Detect) & MTTR (Respond)

In traditional modes, upon alert, admins must manually investigate logs, Google errors, write scripts, test, and schedule downtime... taking hours or days.

The Intelligent Closed-Loop automates the "Investigate-Decide-Code" process via AI, compressing time to minutes.

Scenario 2: Intention Analysis in a Honeypot

What is a Honeypot? Why are old ones "blind"?

A honeypot is a "decoy" system exposed to attract hackers.

Traditional Pain Point: Advanced hackers disable audit services and delete logs immediately upon entry. Traditional security tools based on log files become blind.

New Solution: Tetragon runs in kernel memory. Hackers cannot delete it from user space. Even if they delete files, Tetragon records the *act* of deletion and every command executed in memory.

Scenario 3: Supply Chain Poisoning & Drift Detection

What is a Supply Chain Attack?

When you download an update from an official source, you trust it. But if hackers compromise the software vendor and inject a backdoor into the official update, that is a Supply Chain Attack (e.g., SolarWinds). It exploits "trust" and is hard to defend against.

1. Profiling

Tetragon learns the normal baseline of the software (e.g., only reads /var/log).

2. Drift Detection

After an update, it suddenly tries to read /etc/shadow. This deviates from the baseline! Splunk alerts.

3. Classification

AI analyzes behavior: "Is this a new feature or a backdoor?" Using threat intel, AI identifies it as malicious.
